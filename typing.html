The Great Depression was a severe worldwide economic crisis that began in 1929 after the U.S. stock market crashed. It led to massive unemployment, with about 25% of Americans out of work at the peak of the crisis. Banks collapsed, savings were lost, and businesses shut down, leaving many families in poverty and struggling to survive. Farmers suffered greatly as crop prices dropped, and environmental disasters like the Dust Bowl made things worse in rural areas. The depression spread to other countries, creating a global economic slowdown. In response, U.S. President Franklin D. Roosevelt introduced the New Deal, a series of government programs designed to create jobs, support the poor, and help the economy recover. Although the New Deal helped, the economy did not fully recover until the increased production demands of World War II.
